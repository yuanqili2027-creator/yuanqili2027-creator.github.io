On Reading History

2026-03-18

When I began studying East Asian history, I assumed the difficulty would lie in the sheer volume of names, dates, and dynasties. In practice the harder problem has been learning to read at all — to hold a document at arm's length and ask not only what it says but who wrote it, for whom, and against what.

A textbook flattens a century into a paragraph. A single letter, a tax register, or a diary entry does the opposite: it refuses to summarize itself. Sitting with those smaller documents has taught me that history is less a story already told than a set of arguments still being made, each source pulling in its own direction.

I have also come to distrust the neat arc that connects past to present. It is tempting to read the nineteenth century as a rehearsal for the twentieth, and the twentieth as an explanation for now. But the people I read about did not know how their story would end, and taking their uncertainty seriously seems to me the beginning of reading them fairly.

So these essays are mostly notes to myself — attempts to slow down, to stay with a source a little longer than is comfortable, and to resist the urge to make the past tidier than it was. I expect to be wrong often. That, I am told, is what the first year is for.
